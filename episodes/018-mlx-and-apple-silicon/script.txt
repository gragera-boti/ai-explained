If you've ever tried running an AI model on your own computer, you probably know the drill. You need an expensive NVIDIA GPU, you need CUDA drivers, you need to wrestle with compatibility issues — and if you're on a Mac? Well, until recently, you were mostly out of luck.

But that's changed. And the reason it changed is a framework called MLX.

Let's talk about what MLX is, why it matters, and why running AI on your Mac is suddenly a very real thing.

First, some context. When Apple started designing its own chips — the M1, M2, M3, and now M4 series — they made a decision that turned out to be huge for AI. They built unified memory into the architecture. On a traditional computer, the CPU has its own memory, and the GPU has its own separate memory. If you want to do AI work on the GPU, you first have to copy your data over to the GPU's memory. That transfer takes time. It's a bottleneck.

Apple Silicon doesn't work that way. The CPU and GPU share the same pool of memory. There's no copying back and forth. The data just... sits there, and both processors can access it directly. Think of it like a shared kitchen instead of two separate kitchens with a hallway between them. You don't have to carry ingredients from one to the other. Everything is already within arm's reach.

This is where MLX comes in. MLX is an open-source machine learning framework built by Apple's machine learning research team, released in late 2023. It's designed specifically to take advantage of this unified memory architecture. The name stands for Machine Learning eXploration, and the emphasis is on that last word — exploration. It's built for researchers and developers who want to experiment quickly.

If you've used NumPy for numerical computing in Python, MLX will feel immediately familiar. The API is almost identical. And if you've used PyTorch for building neural networks, you'll recognize the higher-level parts too — things like neural network layers and optimizers follow the same patterns. The learning curve is genuinely small.

But here's what makes MLX different from just running PyTorch on a Mac. MLX was built from the ground up for Apple Silicon. It understands unified memory natively. When you create an array in MLX, it doesn't live on the CPU or the GPU — it lives in shared memory, and operations can happen on either processor without any data transfer. You just write your code, and MLX handles placement efficiently.

MLX also uses lazy evaluation. That means when you write a computation, it doesn't execute immediately. Instead, MLX builds up a graph of what needs to happen and only runs the calculation when you actually need the result. This lets the framework optimize the whole chain of operations together, which can be significantly faster than executing each step one at a time.

So what can you actually do with this? Quite a lot, as it turns out. The most popular use case right now is running large language models locally. There's a companion package called MLX-LM that makes this almost absurdly simple. You can run models like Mistral, LLaMA, Phi, and Qwen directly on your MacBook with a single command. No cloud API, no subscription, no data leaving your machine.

The Hugging Face community has embraced this in a big way. There's an entire MLX Community organization on Hugging Face where people convert and share models optimized for the format. As of now, there are thousands of MLX-compatible models available. You can convert models yourself too — MLX-LM has a built-in conversion tool that can also quantize models to make them smaller and faster.

And it's not just text. The MLX examples repository includes implementations of Stable Diffusion for image generation, Whisper for speech recognition, and various transformer architectures for training your own models. People are using MLX for fine-tuning with LoRA, running vision models, and even doing text-to-speech — all locally, all on Apple hardware.

Now, let's be honest about the limitations. Apple Silicon is impressive, but it's not going to match a workstation with four NVIDIA H100 GPUs. For training large models from scratch, you still want dedicated GPU clusters. The sweet spot for MLX is inference — running pre-trained models — and fine-tuning smaller models or adapters. A MacBook Pro with 36 or 96 gigabytes of unified memory can run surprisingly capable models, including quantized versions of 70-billion parameter models. That's remarkable for a laptop.

The other thing worth noting is that MLX now supports Linux with CUDA as well. It's no longer Mac-only. But its real advantage, its reason for existing, is that deep integration with Apple Silicon's unified memory. On a Mac, it's often the fastest and most efficient option.

There's something philosophically interesting happening here too. For years, AI development was concentrated on expensive cloud infrastructure. You needed access to big servers, big budgets, big companies. MLX is part of a broader movement toward making AI accessible on personal hardware — the computer sitting on your desk, or in your bag. When you can run a capable language model on a laptop without an internet connection, that changes the relationship between people and AI. It becomes a personal tool, not just a cloud service.

So if you have a Mac with Apple Silicon and you've been curious about running AI locally, MLX is where to start. Install it with pip, grab a model from Hugging Face, and you'll be generating text in minutes. No GPU shopping required.

That's MLX and Apple Silicon. Your Mac isn't just for email and spreadsheets anymore — it's a surprisingly capable AI machine, and the tools to use it are free and open source.
