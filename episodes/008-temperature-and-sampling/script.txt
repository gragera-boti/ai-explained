Have you ever noticed that when you ask an AI the same question twice, you sometimes get different answers? Maybe one time it gives you a crisp, factual response, and the next time it wanders off into a creative tangent. That's not a bug. That's temperature and sampling at work — and today, we're going to unpack exactly how they control the personality of AI responses.

Let's start with a fundamental question: how does a language model actually pick its next word?

When a model like GPT or Claude generates text, it doesn't just magically produce sentences. It works one token at a time — token being roughly a word or a piece of a word. At each step, the model looks at everything it's generated so far, plus your prompt, and produces a probability distribution over its entire vocabulary. Tens of thousands of possible next words, each with a score.

Think of it like standing in front of an enormous buffet. Every dish has a little sign showing how appealing it is given what you've already eaten. Some dishes score really high — they're the obvious, expected choices. Others score low — weird, surprising, maybe brilliant, maybe terrible.

Now, the question becomes: how do you pick from that buffet?

The simplest approach is called greedy decoding. You always pick the highest-scoring option. The most probable next word, every single time. It's fast, it's deterministic, and it produces the same output every time you run it. Sounds great, right?

Well, here's the problem. Greedy decoding tends to produce text that's repetitive and bland. It's like always ordering the same dish at every restaurant. Safe, predictable, boring. The model gets stuck in loops, repeating phrases, because the most probable next word after a common phrase is often... the same common phrase again. The Hugging Face team demonstrated this beautifully — a GPT-2 model using greedy search quickly devolved into "I'm not sure if I'll ever be able to" repeated over and over.

So we need something better. Enter sampling.

Instead of always picking the top word, sampling means you randomly select from the probability distribution. Words with higher probabilities are more likely to be chosen, but lower-probability words get a chance too. It's like closing your eyes and pointing at the buffet — you'll probably land on something popular, but there's a chance you'll discover something unexpected.

And this is where temperature comes in. Temperature is a single number — typically between zero and two — that controls how "spread out" or "concentrated" that probability distribution is.

Here's the intuition. Imagine you have three candidate words. Word A has a probability of 70%, Word B has 20%, and Word C has 10%.

At a low temperature — say 0.1 — the model sharpens those differences dramatically. Word A might become 99%, and B and C become nearly zero. The model is extremely confident, extremely predictable. It almost always picks the obvious answer.

At a temperature of 1.0 — the default — the probabilities stay as the model originally computed them. 70, 20, 10. A nice balance of predictability and variety.

At a high temperature — say 2.0 — the distribution flattens out. Now maybe it's 45%, 30%, 25%. Suddenly those unlikely words have a real shot. The output becomes more creative, more surprising, and yes, more prone to nonsense.

The math behind this is elegant. Temperature works by dividing the raw scores — called logits — by the temperature value before passing them through a function called softmax, which converts scores into probabilities. Divide by a small number, and the differences between scores get amplified. Divide by a large number, and they get compressed. It's borrowed from statistical mechanics, actually — the same concept that describes how particles distribute across energy states at different physical temperatures. Hot systems are chaotic and spread out. Cold systems are ordered and concentrated.

But temperature isn't the only knob. There are two other popular sampling strategies worth knowing.

Top-k sampling limits the selection to only the k most probable tokens. If k is 50, the model only considers the top 50 candidates and redistributes their probabilities. Everything else is ignored. It's a simple way to prevent the model from ever picking something truly bizarre, while still allowing some variety.

Then there's top-p sampling, also called nucleus sampling. Instead of picking a fixed number of candidates, you pick the smallest set of tokens whose combined probability exceeds some threshold p. If p is 0.9, you take just enough top tokens to cover 90% of the probability mass. This is more adaptive — when the model is confident, only a few tokens make the cut. When it's uncertain, more tokens qualify. Many practitioners prefer top-p because it adjusts naturally to the situation.

In practice, these techniques are often combined. You might use temperature 0.7 with top-p 0.95. The temperature pulls the distribution slightly toward confident choices, and the top-p filter removes the long tail of weird options. It's like telling the model: "Be creative, but not unhinged."

So when should you use which settings?

For factual tasks — summarization, data extraction, code generation — you want low temperature, maybe 0.0 to 0.3. You want the model to be precise and deterministic. Creativity is not what you need when extracting JSON from a document.

For creative writing, brainstorming, or conversational chat — bump it up. Temperature 0.7 to 1.0 gives you variety without chaos. The model surprises you sometimes, which is exactly what you want.

Go above 1.0 and you're in experimental territory. Good for generating wild ideas or artistic text, but expect some outputs to be incoherent.

And a temperature of exactly zero? That's essentially greedy decoding. The model always picks the most probable token. Fully deterministic, maximally predictable.

Here's something worth noting: when you interact with AI products like ChatGPT or Claude, you usually don't set the temperature yourself. The companies behind these models choose defaults — and sometimes adjust temperature dynamically depending on the task. When you ask for code, the system might quietly lower the temperature. When you ask for a poem, it might raise it. The user experience is shaped by these invisible dials.

Temperature and sampling are a beautiful example of a broader theme in AI: the tension between exploitation and exploration. Do you stick with what you know works, or do you take a chance on something new? Too much exploitation and you get repetitive, boring output. Too much exploration and you get chaos. The art is finding the sweet spot — and that's exactly what these parameters let you tune.

Next time you get a surprising answer from an AI, or a frustratingly repetitive one, you'll know what's happening under the hood. It's not magic. It's just temperature.
